Getting our fill
Thursday, September 05, 2013

We’ve been getting our fill of tomatoes in the past couple of weeks, snatching them up by the armful at the market. They’re at their peak this time of year, ruby-red and juicy, so until the leaves start to change colour, we’re following a largely tomato-based diet. It’s our consolation for summer’s end, and a most effective one at that.

We’ll admit to having eaten tomatoes dressed with olive oil and balsamic as a meal on several occasions, but for those days when we feel like we need something a little more substantial, a simple toasted tomato sandwich does the trick. We toss chopped tomatoes with premium extra-virgin olive oil, a few drops of 18-Year Traditional balsamic vinegar, some slivered basil and some salt, then pile it onto a slice of garlic-rubbed toast. A second slice of toast, set on top with a hearty smash, is optional – a napkin (trust us!) is not.

Now, the above is a perfectly delicious lunch for one or two. But if you have company, especially for dinner, it’s nice to gild the lily a bit. In this case, we strongly suggest you set your oven to 250 degrees and halve some tomatoes, fitting them cut-side-up in a single layer into a casserole dish (or high-rimmed baking sheet). Pour some premium extra-virgin olive oil over, enough to coat the tomatoes and cover the bottom of the dish completely (this is usually about a cup of oil – the tomatoes do not need to be submerged). Sprinkle on some salt and pepper, and send them into the oven for 2.5 hours, flipping them after the first hour and again after the second, so they emerge as they entered, cut-side-up.

They will be burnished and crinkled on the outside, silky and tender within, and will taste like the ripest, sweetest tomatoes imaginable. While still warm, add some sliced garlic, scallions and basil and let cool to room temperature.

Serve the halved tomatoes with toasted bread and goat’s cheese, or chop them and use as a pasta sauce. Sometimes, we just put a spoonful or two on the side of our plate, as a condiment. Store them in a jar in the fridge, covered in the tomato-flavoured oil – you will have some of this wonderfully fragrant oil left over, so keep in mind that it is good for frying eggs, saucing pasta, dipping bread, drizzling over fish or roasting potatoes. Enjoy. 



Precious Cargo
Tuesday, August 20, 2013

Hardly a day goes by without someone asking us about our truffle oil. Some have tasted the wonders of truffle-laced popcorn or aioli at local restaurants and venture in looking to recreate the magic at home. Others have not yet had the good fortune of tasting truffles and come in wondering what all the fuss is about!

Incidentally, the fuss is about one of the most highly prized foodstuffs in the world, one that has inspired both a loyal, eager following of chefs and foodies and a flourishing black market where smugglers will pay up to $10 000 for a pound of precious cargo.

The truffle business is similar to the olive oil business in that much of what is being sold as high-quality white or black truffle is either a chemically altered or manufactured product or an inferior specimen. This drives up the price of real truffles even more, to the point that they can hardly become a staple in most of our kitchens. Luckily, truffle-infused olive oil gives us the flavour of fresh truffle at a fraction of the cost. It is still quite intense and can perfume a dish with just a few drops, so should be used judiciously (but used often, in our opinion!).

Evoolution's white and black truffle oils are all-natural, and made simply by steeping ripe truffles in a mild tasting extra virgin olive oil for extended periods of time.  The olive oil absorbs the flavour and natural oils from the ripe truffles, creating a deep, rich, aromatic truffle-infused olive oil.  Unlike many imitations on the market, our truffle oils contain no artificials flavours, additives or chemicals.

Traditionally, white truffle oil with its sharp, pungent flavour is used for finishing, while black truffle oil, with its deep earthiness, is better suited for cooking.  We use this as a general guideline (defying it when we see fit, of course). A few drops of white truffle oil work well over a salad, a piece of salmon or a slice of buttered toast; black truffle oil is better suited for a cream soup, a potato gratin or a decadent mac and cheese.

Both truffle oils are popular among the staff and we each have our favourite uses for them. Teagan mashes our sharp and pungent white truffle oil with sweet potatoes and yams for a show-stopping side dish. EJ whips our deep, earthy black truffle oil and black truffle salt into cream cheese and sour cream for a delicious dip. Rachel P. slips truffle oil into sautéed potatoes and baked eggs for a decadent brunch. And we all know that truffle popcorn makes a great snack with drinks before dinner!

If you haven't sampled our truffle oils yet, you've surely got plenty of ideas on how to use them now, so make haste! 


Common questions about vinegar
Thursday, August 08, 2013

We love when our customers show an interest in learning more about our oils and vinegars! Here are a couple of the more common questions we get on a daily basis, plus a reason to buy that extra bottle of balsamic on your next visit. Click on the links at the bottom for more information!

What is “Mother”?

“Mother” of vinegar will naturally occur in vinegar products as the result of the vinegar bacteria itself. Mother is actually cellulose (a natural carbohydrate which is the fiber in foods like celery and lettuce) produced by the harmless vinegar bacteria. Today, most manufacturers pasteurize their product before bottling to prevent these bacteria from forming “mother” while sitting on the shelf.

After opening, you may notice “mother” beginning to form. Vinegar containing “mother” is not harmful or spoiled – you can continue to use and enjoy this product!

How Long Does Vinegar Last?

The Vinegar Institute conducted studies to find out, and confirmed that vinegar’s shelf life is almost indefinite. Because of its acid nature, vinegar is self-preserving and does not need refrigeration. White distilled vinegar will remain virtually unchanged over an extended period of time. And, while some changes can be observed in other types of vinegars, such as color changes or the development of a haze or sediment, this is only an aesthetic change. The product can still be used and enjoyed with confidence.

Olive oil crust for summer
Monday, July 29, 2013

We’ve developed a bit of a habit, lately, of buying too much fruit. Every week, without fail, we get a little too excited at the market and end up with more fruit than we know what to do with. Of course, this is more of a blessing than a problem! A big salad always helps, keeping us in lunches all week, but we’d be hard-pressed to find a better treatment for summer fruit than a flaky crust and a hot oven.

A simple, rustic fruit tart is a beautiful, underrated way to end a summer meal; with no bells or whistles in sight, the flavour is pure fruit, intensified. This particular tart is nothing more than apricots, sugar and a touch of Blenheim Apricot Balsamic, tossed together and baked into a lovely olive oil tart base. The tart crust is a breeze to make (with no chilling or melting of butter) and it is a perfect complement to fresh fruit. Not much else is required! Of course, on the second round, we couldn’t help but gild the lily with some candied pistachios scattered overtop – but having had both versions, we can confidently say that it is just as good without.  

This olive oil tart crust is a good, basic crust for any fruit tart. Use whatever you have! Recipe for the crust is here. 



Fresh oil
Thursday, July 18, 2013

We have a brand-new varietal olive oil in the shop – an extremely fresh Barnea from Peru! It’s the first of our southern hemisphere oils, and it’s a robust oil with plenty of bitterness and pepper. If you’ve enjoyed our Barnea oil in the past, you’ll love this one!

This particular oil is an early harvest oil, pressed from unripe green olives. The difference between an early harvest and late harvest Barnea can be astonishing. Visually, an early harvest oil will have a greenish tinge to it, while a late harvest oil, pressed from now-black olives, will have a golden hue. In terms of taste, the early harvest will be ‘green’, peppery and bitter in taste, while a late harvest will be sweeter and more buttery.

We’ve also got a new fused oil – Baklouti Green Chili! It packs more heat than the Harissa and Chipotle, so we’ve ranked them for your convenience. Instead of a slow burn, this fiery oil hits you up front; it would be great on eggs, pizza and grilled meat or veg. We’re thinking green chili-lime-cilantro corn on the cob is in our future!

Picnic season
Monday, July 01, 2013

Another long weekend has come and gone, and after a wonderful Canada Day, the workweek may look less appealing than ever. The thing about summer in Edmonton, though, is that every warm, sunny day feels fortuitous. As far as we’re concerned, July and August are like one big, long weekend! We put aside our work for a little while each day and head outdoors, soaking in as much sun as we can.

To take advantage of the clear skies, we’ve been picnicking a lot lately – whether it’s during the lunch hour or after a long walk in the evening, there’s nothing like sitting cross-legged in the grass with good friends and good food. Our favourite meals are the spontaneous ones, such as this, where quality ingredients are thrown together and everyone can nibble away to their heart’s content. A nice salad, with some bread and fruit and cheese, is all you need – provided there is something cold and sparkling to be imbibed alongside.

This grainy salad lends itself to endless variations, with a base of wheatberries and quinoa spruced up with Rainier cherries, arugula, pistachios and pickled radishes. Often, we find ourselves with a little bit of this and that left toward the end of the week – a handful of strawberries, half a bag of greens, etc. – so this is a great way to clear out the fridge before the next market day, and it’s delicious to boot! Roasted Rhubarb
Monday, June 03, 2013

We brought home a few stalks of our favourite fruit-that’s-actually-a-vegetable from the market yesterday and couldn’t wait to use them. Pie, cake and crumble all have their place, but for our first dose of the season, we preferred to keep it simple.

Nothing brightens up a rainy day like rosy-hued rhubarb, roasted with rich vanilla and balsamic vinegar.

Two bunches (2 lbs) of rhubarb were cut into 2-inch segments and tossed with ½ cup Silk Road vanilla sugar, ½ cup Cara Cara Orange Vanilla white balsamic and a big squeeze of lemon.  Onto a sheet pan they went, to be roasted in the oven at 350 degrees for about 30 minutes, until tender and coated in a sweet-tart syrup.

Speaking from experience, this is as lovely for breakfast with some yogurt and granola as it is straight from the fridge as an afternoon pick-me-up. The heady aroma of citrus and vanilla wafting from the oven is pure comfort, at any time of day.

Any of our fruity balsamics, white or dark, would work well here. In future, we hope to fold it through custard, use it to top meringues, spoon over cheesecake or even tone down the sugar a bit and use it as a savory condiment.
