Oil and vinegar - emulsified
Friday, November 08, 2013

The backbone of any salad is the dressing; so how do you make yours perfect every time? Make sure to emulsify! An emulsion is a homogenous mixture of two or more liquids that normally cannot be blended. For our purposes, oil and vinegar are a good example. 

Image courtesy of Summer Tomato

To blend the two together, an emulsifying agent is necessary. Emulsifying agents such as mustard, egg yolk or honey contain both a water-soluble component and an oil-soluble component, lowering the surface tension between the two liquids and encouraging the suspension of one liquid in another. 

Image courtesy of Cooking Light

If you’d like to dress your salad in advance, emulsifying is crucial – without it, the dressing will separate, the oil clinging to the salad leaves and the vinegar sinking to the bottom. Overall, an emulsified vinaigrette will coat your salad better and keep it fresher when dressed; it will also keep longer on its own, in a jar in the fridge.

Image courtesy of Bon Appétit

Here is a simple formula for vinaigrette (mustard is our favourite emulsifier, but an equal amount of egg yolk, honey or mayonnaise will also work).  Whisk 3 tbsp. of balsamic vinegar with 1 tbsp. of mustard and a big pinch of salt; whisking slowly, stream in ½ cup of extra-virgin olive oil. Add fresh herbs and/or chopped shallots to taste. This can also be done in a blender. Enjoy!

Olive Oil Body Scrub
Tuesday, October 15, 2013

Well, Thanksgiving has come and gone! We hope yours was splendid and full of good food. We thought we’d take a breather from the recipes this week (though feel free to check out the new additions to our recipe section) and offer you a few other tips on using our oils.  Our range of Olivella products is much-loved among the staff and customers alike; what you may not know is that in addition to these products, many of us also use pure extra-virgin olive oil in our skin/haircare regimes. Here are a few ways to do the same:

  •  comb a small amount of Blood Orange oil through your hair to keep it smooth and shiny (this is especially helpful for curly hair)
  • dab some olive oil onto a cotton swab and remove your makeup
  • keep a bottle of olive oil next to your sink; after washing dishes, rub a small amount into your hands to moisturize
  • ditch the lip balm and smooth some olive oil onto your lips instead
  • mix one of our varietal olive oils in a 2:1 ratio with castor oil and try out the popular oil-cleansing method


Our personal favourite is a super-simple, inexpensive olive oil body scrub. Combine ½ cup brown sugar with 2 tbsp. olive oil and ¼ cup baking soda. Apply a small amount to wet skin, scrub very gently and rinse away! It leaves your skin incredibly soft and smooth and can be stored neatly in a jar in your shower. We love using our Eureka Lemon oil for a fresh scent! 


Eminently worthy
Thursday, October 10, 2013

Thanksgiving is nearly upon us, and we’ve got a few suggestions for sprucing up your recipes this year…slip some Herbes de Provence olive oil under the skin of your turkey, splash Cranberry Pear balsamic into your cranberry sauce and mix some Wild Mushroom & Sage olive oil into your stuffing. With Garlic mashed potatoes and a big green salad, you’re well on your way to an Evoolution-inspired Thanksgiving.

But don’t think we forgot about dessert! There’s been talk of Vermont Maple pecan tarts and Honey Ginger pumpkin pie, but imagine our surprise when last week, in an attempt to save some teeny-tiny market pears from the compost bin (yes, we’re still buying too much fruit), we wound up with a pear sauce eminently worthy of our Thanksgiving table.

It’s the simplest thing, really, the kind of sauce you can make half-asleep on Sunday morning (which is always handy). Take the best pears you can find, then peel, core and quarter them. We used little Seckel pears from the City Market, which have firm flesh and exceptional sweetness, and we recommend you do the same. Tumble them into a pot with some sugar, a pinch of salt and a trickling of Cinnamon Pear balsamic. Cook for a long time over low-ish heat, stirring occasionally, but mostly letting them do their thing while you inhale the fragrance of caramelizing sugar.

After a couple of hours, you have Caramelized Pear Sauce – the more complex, elegant cousin of plain-Jane applesauce – to be spooned over ice cream, layered into trifle or eaten with plain whole-milk yogurt for a swoon-worthy breakfast.  This weekend, we’ll be serving a dollop alongside buttery buckwheat cake and salted caramel whipped cream, and a happy Thanksgiving it shall be.

Free Fatty Acids & Smoking Point of EVOO
Thursday, September 26, 2013

If you've ever wondered about the significance of the FFA (Free Fatty Acid) content we include on our varietal cards, here's a simple explanation:

Free Fatty Acids in olive oil are produced through the breakdown of triglycerides (ie. fats and oils), which are composed of one molecule of glycerol and three fatty acids. This process is called lipolysis, and it occurs when the cell structure of the olive is damaged. As olives are crushed, lipolysis will always occur to some extent, but it can be minimized by using care when handling the olives and not letting them sit for too long between picking and crushing.

Image courtesy of The Guardian

So an oil pressed from olives of poor quality, or one that has been carelessly extracted, will suffer significant breakdown and have a higher FFA content; an oil pressed with care, from high-quality olives will suffer minimal breakdown and have a lower FFA content, which is desirable.

It is important to note that FFA content does not affect flavour, but that it does affect smoking point. Generally speaking, the lower the FFAs, the higher the smoking point – a high-quality, low FFA extra-virgin olive oil retains more complex bonds and is therefore more resistant to oxidation.


Image courtesy of California Olive Ranch

Some important numbers:

- An olive oil must have an FFA content of 0.8 or less to be considered extra-virgin

- The lowest FFA content possible in an extra-virgin olive oil, without refinement, is 0.18

- All of the oils we offer have an FFA content of 0.4 or less, as they are crushed from quality olives within 4 hours of being picked

Something to take away:

Though you may have heard that extra-virgin olive oil should not be used for cooking, if you use a fresh oil with a low FFA content, you may cook with it as you please. Our staff members use our varietals for pretty much everything, from high-heat cooking to drizzling over salads, and it is perfectly acceptable (and delicious) to do the same.
